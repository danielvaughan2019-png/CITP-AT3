Background

Lama Lama Country, the energy shortage, and the impact on residents

Context of the Problem

Lama Lama Country is a culturally rich and natural piece of land that belongs to the Lama Lama people (EWB Challenge, 2026). The land stretches from Princess Charlotte Bay to Eastern Cape York Peninsula. Due to the Lama Lama People's values and beliefs, and the land being remote and surrounded by wilderness, resources such as electricity are limited within this area (Yintjingga Aboriginal Corporation, n.d.).

As a result of this, the Lama Lama economy cannot expand, and outstations cannot be upgraded due to the current power demand for these outstations being too high. Therefore, a new design or upgrade to the current energy system must be made for outstations to have a reliable power source, and extra energy for potential upgrades. Doing so could save the people money, time, and adhere to their values of caring for the land they have.

Significance to Stakeholders

Due to their geographic isolation, importing or purchasing third party resources, this places an unsustainable burden on the community as during tropical wet season, or other extreme weather events like severe flooding, it can threaten critical day-to-day operations and community resilience when access to third party resources is cut off (Bushlight, 2011) (Yintjingga Aboriginal Corporation, n.d.). Environmentally and medically, the lack of a modern source of energy/resources also places restrictions on the communities needs. The power demand (with the current system in place) will then become a burden if residents from elsewhere want to tour or move back into Lama Lama Country.

What is Already Being Done?

The current energy system consists of 2 hybrid systems. This consists of two 8kVa diesel generators, one at Moojeba outstation, the other at Theethinji outstation (Bushlight, 2011). They also do have a small amount of solar arrays connected for a small output of a renewable source of energy (Bushlight, 2011). One heavy limitation of this is no adaptability and flexibility. If upgrades were put into the outstations to use more energy, the current output of energy might not be able to keep up with the power demand. Another heavy limitation of this is the reliance on third party resources (diesel) is a lose-lose situation. If the resources are cut off, not enough power would be available to keep up with outstation needs. If the diesel is burnt for power, it burns dirty and damages their environment, compromising their cultural values in the process.

Why This is Not Enough

The central limitation of this goes back to the lose-lose situation of using a diesel generator (Bushlight 2011). Firstly, it requires diesel imported by a third-party resource into Lama Lama country. Concerns can also arise if the generator were to stop working, putting the Lama Lama community without a manual source of energy. Secondly, using diesel as a fuel to gain energy pollutes the environment in the process, compromising their cultural values with the land.

What Your Project Aims to Achieve

Our solution to this is a microgrid system that consists of solar panels and Lithium-Phosphate batteries. This solution gives the Lama Lama residents a renewable, eco-friendly, source of energy, which also allows outstation rangers to perform their duties whilst the batteries charge. Ideally, rangers would be trained to operate, and repair the microgrid system.
