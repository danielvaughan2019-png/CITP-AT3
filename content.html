Tutorial / Zone:
Tutorial 1 - Aaron Ngan
Group Name:
LumenPower

This project addresses the energy infrastructure needs of Port Stewart, the remote outstation home of the Lama Lama people on Lama Lama Country, Cape York Peninsula, Queensland. The community currently relies on ageing diesel generation supplemented by limited solar, a configuration that is costly, environmentally harmful, and vulnerable to supply disruption. LumenPower's proposed solution is a hybrid smart energy management system that integrates new solar panels, a dual battery bank, and an intelligent forecasting algorithm to reduce diesel dependence, improve energy reliability, and build a foundation for long-term community energy independence.

Context

Port Stewart is situated near the mouth of the Stewart River on the northern shores of Princess Charlotte Bay, Cape York Peninsula, in Far North Queensland (Centre for Appropriate Technology [CfAT], 2021). It is the traditional home of the Lama Lama people - the Traditional Owners of lands extending several hundred kilometres around Princess Charlotte Bay - who describe themselves as "sand beach people" and "saltwater people," with a profound cultural connection to sea country, coastal landscapes, and marine resources (Yintjingga Aboriginal Corporation [YAC], 2026). The community is among the most geographically isolated in Australia: the nearest regional centre, Coen, is an eight-hour four-wheel-drive journey accessible only during the dry season, and the community is effectively cut off for the approximately five months of the wet season each year (Engineers Without Borders Australia [EWB], 2025). This isolation makes reliable energy infrastructure not merely a convenience but a prerequisite for the community's safety, cultural programs, ranger operations, and year-round presence on Country.

Like many remote First Nations outstations across Australia, Port Stewart's energy system is heavily reliant on diesel generation. Nationally, over 100,000 Indigenous Australians live in off-grid remote communities where diesel generators remain the primary power source, often at costs exceeding $5,000 per quarter (Stellae Energy, 2022; Renew Magazine, 2020). In the Cape York context, fuel must be freighted by road or barge, making supply chains fragile and prices significantly higher than urban centres (Cape York Weekly, 2026). Port Stewart's tropical environment - characterised by extreme heat, high humidity, cyclonic conditions, and pest pressure - accelerates equipment degradation and compounds maintenance challenges for communities with limited access to specialist tradespeople (EWB Challenge, 2026).

Design Area & Project Opportunity

LumenPower selected Design Area 3 - Energy, specifically Project Opportunity 3.2: Microgrids and hybrid systems for outstations. This opportunity calls for the design of modular solar and storage systems that can scale, be repaired, and operate safely in heat, humidity, and pest-prone environments (EWB Challenge, 2026). The group was drawn to this area because energy underpins every other aspect of life at Port Stewart - from refrigeration for food and medicine, to communications, to the operation of the ranger program that is central to Lama Lama cultural and land management practice. Without reliable power, the community's ability to live on and care for Country is constrained in a fundamental way. A well-designed energy system creates a platform on which every other community priority can build.

Needs Statement

How might we design a modular, smart solar and storage system that reliably powers the Port Stewart outstation through the extremes of the wet and dry season, reduces dependence on diesel, and can be maintained and expanded by the Lama Lama community over time? This statement was developed through analysis of the EWB Challenge Design Brief, the community's expressed values around caring for Country and cultural self-determination, and the practical engineering constraints of the Port Stewart environment - including seasonal isolation, tropical climate, and limited access to specialist maintenance (YAC, 2026; EWB Challenge, 2026). It reflects the community's desire for a system that is not only functional but genuinely theirs to own, operate, and adapt.

Background

Empathy Stage Research & Evidence

This section summarises the most relevant background information related to Design Area 3 - Energy, and Project Opportunity 3.2 - Microgrids and hybrid systems for outstations. It draws on the team’s individual background research to present a cohesive picture of the problem, its significance, the current state of energy infrastructure at Port Stewart, and what LumenPower’s design aims to achieve.

Context of the Problem

Lama Lama Country is a culturally rich and ecologically significant area belonging to the Lama Lama people, stretching from Princess Charlotte Bay to the eastern Cape York Peninsula (EWB Challenge, 2026). Due to the Lama Lama people’s values and beliefs, and the land being remote and surrounded by wilderness, essential resources such as electricity are limited within this area (Yintjingga Aboriginal Corporation [YAC], n.d.). As a result of this isolation, the Lama Lama economy cannot readily expand, and outstations cannot be upgraded because the current energy system cannot support the increased power demand that improvements would require. A new design or meaningful upgrade to the existing energy infrastructure is therefore necessary if outstations are to have a reliable power source and the capacity to accommodate future development while remaining true to the community’s values of caring for Country.

Significance to Stakeholders

Due to Port Stewart’s geographic isolation, the community depends on imported third-party resources - most critically, diesel fuel - to power its energy system. This dependency places an unsustainable burden on the community, particularly during the tropical wet season or other extreme weather events such as severe flooding, when access to those resources is cut off and critical day-to-day operations and community resilience are directly threatened (Bushlight, 2011; YAC, n.d.). Environmentally and medically, the absence of a modern, reliable energy source also places restrictions on the community’s needs - limiting refrigeration for food and medicine, communications, and the operation of the ranger program. Looking ahead, the power demand constraints of the current system will become an even greater burden if residents from elsewhere seek to return to Lama Lama Country or if visitor and tourism activity on Country grows.

What is Already Being Done?

The existing energy system at Port Stewart consists of two hybrid configurations: an 8 kVA diesel generator at Moojeba outstation and another at Theethinji outstation, each supplemented by a small solar array providing a limited renewable input (Bushlight, 2011). While this setup represents an initial step toward hybrid generation, it has significant structural limitations. The system has no adaptability or flexibility - if outstation upgrades were made that increased energy consumption, the current output could not keep pace with the higher power demand. More critically, the heavy reliance on diesel imports represents a lose-lose situation: if fuel supply is disrupted, there is insufficient power to sustain outstation operations; if diesel is burned continuously, it produces emissions that damage the very environment the Lama Lama people are committed to protecting, directly compromising their cultural values (Bushlight, 2011).

Why This is Not Enough

The central limitation of the existing system is its structural dependence on diesel as a primary fuel source. Diesel must be imported by third-party suppliers into Lama Lama Country at a cost of approximately $5,000 annually, a figure that does not account for the logistical complexity and risk of wet season supply disruption (Bushlight, 2011). Should the generators fail, the community is left without any independent source of power. Beyond cost and supply risk, the ongoing combustion of diesel pollutes the local environment, placing the community’s energy needs in direct tension with their deeply held cultural obligation to care for Country. The existing solar arrays, while a positive step, are too small in capacity and too simple in their control logic to meaningfully reduce diesel dependence or to adapt to changing load conditions. The system, as it stands, cannot support the community’s aspirations for sustainable growth and greater self-determination.

What Your Project Aims to Achieve

LumenPower’s solution is a hybrid microgrid system centred on new high-efficiency solar panels and a LiFePO₄ lithium battery bank, integrated with the existing infrastructure through a smart energy management algorithm. This approach gives the Lama Lama community a renewable, environmentally responsible source of energy that aligns with their values, while the intelligent control system ensures the existing diesel generator is retained only as an emergency fallback rather than a primary source. Ranger staff would be trained to operate and carry out basic maintenance on the system, building genuine technical capacity within the community and reducing long-term dependence on outside expertise. The result is a design that is not only technically sound but also culturally grounded, scalable, and sustainable over the long term.

Section 6

Detailed Design

The selected design - a hybrid smart energy management system - integrates new solar and battery infrastructure with the existing solar array and diesel generator, guided by a real-time AI-driven monitoring and balancing system. The design philosophy was shaped by both Lama Lama community Values and EWB design brief prioritising long lifecycle interconnected design and cost (EWB Challenge Design Considerations, 2026).

System Components

The final proposed design consists of the following key components:

  • Monocrystalline solar panels - new high-efficiency panels supplementing the existing array
  • LiFePO₄ (lithium iron phosphate) battery array - new primary battery storage with long cycle life and thermal stability
  • MPPT charge controller - maximises energy harvested from the solar array
  • Inverter - converts DC stored energy to AC for appliance use
  • DC bus / combiner - integrates multiple energy sources onto a common bus
  • Bypass switch - allows direct solar-to-inverter routing when batteries are full
  • Smart energy monitoring and balancing system - the core intelligence layer of the design

Redundancy & Resilience

A key strength of this design is its multiple layers of redundancy. The system is capable of drawing power from three independent sources, in order of preference:

  • 1. New LiFePO₄ battery array - primary storage, optimised for efficiency
  • 2. Old battery system - retained as secondary storage and backup
  • 3. Diesel generator - emergency fallback, with significantly reduced usage compared to the original system

This layered approach creates a highly resilient system suited to the remote conditions of Lama Lama Country, where maintenance access is limited and downtime is unacceptable.

How the Smart System Works

This is the key part of the system that sets it apart and makes it the most practical for the Lama Lama energy needs.

The smart monitoring systems core component is a set of mirrored computers (onsite and offsite). That provide an output based on the following inputs:

  • Battery information - current state of charge, tempreture, cycle count
  • Current energy generation - live solar input
  • Number of users - active load on the system
  • Past energy usage patterns - historical data of energy usage
  • Weather - forcast for predicting solar yield
  • Time of day - anticipating peak and off-peak demand periods

Using this data, the algorithm makes two key real-time decisions:

1. Solar Power Distribution - Where to Put Incoming Energy

The system selects the optimal destination for incoming solar power, choosing from:

  • New LiFePO₄ battery array (primary storage)
  • Old battery system (secondary storage)
  • Bypass directly to inverter (when batteries are full)

2. Energy Supply Selection - Where to Draw Power From

This system choices which of the folowing is the optimal sources to take energyfrom:

  • New LiFePO₄ battery array
  • Old battery array
  • Direct from solar array
  • Diesel generator (emergency backup only)

Additionally, the system sends notifications to the community ranger's informing them of predicted energy lulls and surpluses, or when the generator is activated, enabling proactive maintenance and oversight.

The result is a system that delivers improved efficiency through intelligent prioritisation, greater adaptation to changing conditions, improved longevity of all components through optimised charge cycles, and better utilisation of all available energy resources - both new and existing.

By prioritising renewable solar energy, the design respects and reflects this ethos. Section 7

Prototyping

Given the remote and resource-constrained nature of Port Stewart, LumenPower's prototyping approach was theoretical and algorithmic rather than physical. The core intelligence of the design - the smart energy management algorithm - was the highest-risk and highest-value component to validate, as the entire system's performance depends on its ability to make correct routing decisions across a wide range of real-world energy and weather conditions. Physical hardware components such as the solar panels, inverter, and battery banks are commercially proven products whose individual performance is well established; it was the integration logic and decision-making behaviour of the algorithm that required theoretical prototyping and stress-testing.

What Was Prototyped & Why

The team prototyped the smart energy management algorithm - specifically its decision logic for routing incoming solar power and selecting the active energy source under varying forecast and battery state-of-charge (SOC) conditions. This was prioritised because algorithm failure in a remote outstation context has immediate consequences: incorrect routing decisions could deplete the battery banks, trigger unnecessary diesel generator runtime, or fail to protect batteries from damaging deep discharge. Validating the decision tree theoretically allowed the team to identify edge cases and refine thresholds before committing them to a hardware specification.

Construction Process

The prototype was developed as a theoretical simulation using a rule-based decision algorithm modelled in a spreadsheet environment. Input parameters were defined for five key variables: solar irradiance (modelled across dry season, wet season, and storm day profiles), lithium battery SOC (0–100%), lead acid battery SOC (0–100%), estimated load demand (kWh/day), and a 48-hour weather forecast category (full sun, partly cloudy, extended overcast, storm/mixed, dry season clear run). For each combination of inputs, the algorithm was stepped through its decision tree and the expected output recorded - where to route solar power, which bank to draw from, whether to activate the diesel generator, and whether to trigger a ranger alert. Approximately 30 distinct input scenarios were tested across three passes, with parameters adjusted between passes to simulate seasonal transitions and edge cases such as simultaneous low SOC in both banks during a multi-day storm event.

Testing Method & Results

Testing was conducted through algorithmic simulation across 30 scenarios, each representing a distinct combination of weather forecast category, lithium SOC, lead acid SOC, and daily load demand. Each scenario was evaluated against three criteria: (1) whether the correct energy source was selected given the SOC levels and forecast, (2) whether battery protection thresholds were respected - specifically the lithium 20% cut-off and lead acid 50% depth-of-discharge limit - and (3) whether the ranger alert and diesel activation triggers fired at the correct SOC thresholds. Section 8

Implementation Plan

The implementation of the LumenPower hybrid smart energy system is structured across six stages, spanning initial construction through to long-term community ownership. Given Port Stewart’s seasonal inaccessibility - the community is effectively cut off during the wet season, which spans approximately November to March - all on-site work must be scheduled during the dry season window. Community involvement is embedded at every stage, reflecting the Lama Lama people’s right to self-determination and their deep knowledge of Country and its conditions.

  • Stage 1 - Construction & Community Training

    Installation will be carried out by two CEC-accredited electricians based in Cairns, mobilised during the dry season when road access to Port Stewart is available. Community rangers and nominated members of Yintjingga Aboriginal Corporation (YAC) will be involved directly in the installation process - assisting with panel mounting, cable routing, and equipment placement - to build hands-on familiarity with the system from day one. Alongside physical installation, a two-day training program will be delivered covering basic system monitoring via the Victron Cerbo GX interface, how to interpret ranger notification alerts, how to operate the diesel generator manually if automatic start fails, and first-line troubleshooting procedures for common faults such as tripped isolators or low battery warnings.

  • Stage 2 - Introduction to Client & Community

    Prior to installation, LumenPower will present the finalised system design to YAC leadership and community Elders in a co-design review session, ensuring the community has full visibility of what is being installed, why each component is included, and how the system will behave across different seasonal conditions. Plain-language system guides - including a one-page visual summary of the algorithm’s decision logic in dry season, wet season, and storm scenarios - will be produced and left with the community in both printed and digital formats. Once installed and commissioned, a one-week supervised handover period will allow community members to operate the system with the installer team on-site for support before independent operation begins.

  • Stage 3 - Long-term Use, Management & Maintenance

    Day-to-day system monitoring will be managed by trained community rangers, supported by the Victron VRM cloud monitoring platform which enables remote oversight by EWB or YAC technical staff from outside Port Stewart. An annual maintenance visit by a Cairns-based solar technician - scheduled at the start of each dry season - will cover panel cleaning, connection integrity checks, BMS calibration, lead acid equalisation, generator servicing, and software updates to the smart controller. The LiFePO₄ battery bank has an expected cycle life of 6,500 cycles and a projected service life of 15 or more years under the Port Stewart load profile; the lead acid reserve bank should be budgeted for replacement every five to seven years given the tropical heat.

  • Stage 4 - Evaluation

    System performance will be evaluated at six months, twelve months, and annually thereafter against four key indicators: diesel generator runtime hours per month (target: reduction of at least 70% compared to the pre-installation baseline), average daily battery SOC at sunrise (target: above 60% for lithium bank), number of ranger alert notifications triggered per month (target: fewer than two per month outside the peak wet season), and community-reported satisfaction with power reliability. Data will be drawn automatically from the Victron VRM platform and reviewed jointly by YAC and EWB at each evaluation point. Any algorithm threshold adjustments identified through real-world performance data will be implemented remotely via the smart controller’s configuration interface.

  • Stage 5 - Community Ownership

    Full operational ownership transfers to YAC at the conclusion of the supervised handover period at the end of Stage 2. The system is designed to minimise dependency on external expertise: the smart algorithm operates autonomously, the Victron ecosystem provides remote diagnostics accessible to any authorised user with internet access, and all components are standard Australian-market products with distributor networks in Cairns and Townsville. To build technical capacity within the community over time, LumenPower recommends that YAC sponsor one or two community members to complete a Certificate II in Rural Operations or equivalent training that includes solar system maintenance, enabling increasingly self-sufficient upkeep without the need for annual technician visits in future years.

  • Stage 6 - Repair & Contingency

    The system’s layered redundancy - lithium bank, lead acid bank, and diesel generator - means that no single component failure results in a complete loss of power. Replacement parts for all components are available through Cairns-based suppliers within standard delivery timeframes, and the modular design allows individual components to be swapped without taking the entire system offline. For wet season failures when road access is unavailable, the diesel generator provides emergency backup on the 200-litre onsite fuel tank, and a stock of critical spare fuses, isolators, and a spare Victron BMV battery monitor will be held on-site. Remote diagnosis via VRM can guide community rangers through first-line repairs under instruction from a technical advisor by phone or satellite communications.

Future Impact on the Community

Reliable, affordable energy has transformative potential for the Lama Lama community beyond the immediate reduction in diesel costs. Consistent power enables year-round refrigeration for food and medicine storage, more effective operation of the ranger program - including communications, charging of monitoring equipment, and lighting for ranger bases - and improved conditions for community gatherings, education, and cultural activities on Country. The reduction in diesel freight also decreases the community’s exposure to global fuel price volatility and the logistical risks of remote supply chains, both of which have been identified as critical vulnerabilities for Cape York communities (Cape York Weekly, 2026). One potential unintended consequence is an increase in electricity demand as reliability improves and community members acquire additional appliances; this can be mitigated by sizing the system with future expansion in mind - the modular LiFePO₄ battery architecture and the MPPT controller’s spare capacity allow additional panels and battery modules to be added incrementally as demand grows, without replacing the core system.

Cost Narrative

The estimated system total of $52,489 AUD sits within the expected range for a comparable off-grid solar upgrade in remote Queensland, where grid connection alone can cost between $30,000 and $100,000 for rural properties (SPS Energy, 2025). The two dominant cost drivers, installation labour ($14,000) and the new LiFePO₄ battery bank ($5,999), together account for approximately 38% of the upfront budget, which is consistent with industry norms for remote tropical deployments (The Off-Grid Shop, 2025). Critically, the hybrid integration approach avoids the cost of replacing the existing diesel generator and lead acid bank entirely, reducing upfront expenditure significantly relative to Options 3.1 and 3.2. The most significant ongoing cost driver is lead acid battery replacement every five to seven years under Cape York's tropical heat, which accelerates degradation compared to temperate climates and should be budgeted for from the outset (IndexBox Australia, 2026). Opportunities to reduce cost include engaging TAFE trained local community members for panel cleaning and first line maintenance, sourcing components through Queensland Government bulk procurement channels available to Indigenous councils, and phasing the smart controller software configuration as Stage 2 once the core hardware is operational.

Funding & Financial Sustainability

Several funding pathways are available to substantially offset project costs. The federal Small-scale Technology Certificates (STC) scheme provides an estimated $3,500–$5,000 upfront rebate on a 10 kW system in Queensland (SolarQuotes, 2026), and the Cheaper Home Batteries Program, introduced in August 2025, provides a further $1,000–$3,000 reduction on compliant battery storage (SolarScorecard, 2026). The 2025–26 Indigenous Councils Funding Program (ICFP) allocates $74.6 million across Queensland's 16 Indigenous councils for local government services, with energy infrastructure listed as an eligible category (Department of Local Government, Water and Volunteers [DLGWV], 2025). Of particular relevance, the National Emergency Management Agency's Queensland Resilience and Risk Reduction Fund (QRRRF) has already funded solar and communications infrastructure at Pormpuraaw Aboriginal Shire Council to the value of $206,000, establishing a direct precedent for this type of project in the Cape York context (National Emergency Management Agency [NEMA], 2024). Combined, these sources could reduce the community's net project cost by 30–50%. The system does not generate direct community income; however, it eliminates the majority of diesel fuel expenditure and substantially reduces electricity costs, projecting a saving of $2,000–$3,000 AUD annually per household (The Off Grid Shop, 2025), with full cost recovery estimated within 8–12 years and effectively free power for the remaining system life of 15 or more years.

Section 10

Other Considerations

Social, Ethical, and Operational Dimensions

Beyond the core technical and financial metrics, the long-term success of the hybrid smart energy system hinges on respecting the seasonal rhythms and cultural protocols of the Lama Lama people. Prioritising local employment during the installation phase ensures that technical knowledge remains embedded within the community, turning an infrastructure upgrade into a sustainable skills-development opportunity. Operationally, the system's reliance on a continuous internet link for offsite data mirroring introduces minor data privacy considerations; all energy usage data must be managed transparently in partnership with the Yintjingga Aboriginal Corporation (YAC) to preserve community sovereignty over their infrastructure data. Furthermore, because energy reliability will drastically improve, gradual social shifts in appliance usage must be monitored through community-led demand awareness, ensuring the modular system is expanded before peak thresholds are consistently crossed.

Community Reaction - Journey Map

The journey map traces the likely community experience from first awareness through to long-term adoption and operational self-determination.

  • 1. Awareness: Initial introduction of the hybrid smart system design to YAC leadership and Elders during co-design review sessions, building trust by emphasizing the reuse and value-add of existing assets.
  • 2. Engagement: Active participation of community rangers in pre-installation planning, refining the automated alert parameters and clarifying how notifications are delivered via satellite links.
  • 3. Trialling: A supervised handover period where rangers operate the physical system alongside the installation team, adapting to the automated balancing logic between the new solar array and existing generators.
  • 4. Adoption: Routine daily monitoring by the rangers using local system interfaces, characterized by a stabilized power grid and an immediate 70% reduction in manual diesel refuelling chores.
  • 5. Ownership: Full technical self-determination, where YAC independently coordinates seasonal dry season maintenance and resolves minor line faults as true custodians of their energy grid.
Section 11

Recommendations

This section identifies further actionable activities outside the immediate engineering design scope that the client and community should undertake to ensure long-term operational success.

Further Activity Required by the Client

  • Formalize YAC Co-Design Review: Conduct a dedicated community presentation and feedback session with Lama Lama Elders and YAC leadership to approve the final physical placement of the supplementary solar panels and battery enclosures prior to dry-season mobilization.
  • Establish a Ranger Training Framework: Coordinate with regional technical training providers to sponsor selected community land rangers for localized solar maintenance micro-credentials to formalize first-line troubleshooting capabilities.
  • Procure Telecommunications Agreements: Secure stable data plan contracts for the Starlink or Telstra 4G uplink dedicated to the system's smart remote cloud monitoring interface before the wet season isolation window.
  • Execute Structural and Cyclone-Rating Assessments: Verify that the existing outstation structures or ground-mount areas at Moojeba and Theethinji can safely support the aluminium tilt-frame racking under Region C cyclonic conditions.

Further Research Needed

Additional research is required to map the granular, appliance-level energy load profiles of the Port Stewart outstations during the peak cultural and tourism periods of the dry season. Because historical data is limited, tracking current consumption habits will help refine the smart energy algorithm's predictive weighting for user load demands. This field research is best conducted by a collaborative partnership between YAC rangers and an EWB technical advisor using temporary data-logging clamp meters on the existing distribution boards over a two-week high-occupancy window.
