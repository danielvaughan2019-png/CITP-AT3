Section 3

Problem Description & Design Criteria

User Needs Statement: How might we develop a reliable and cost-effective energy solution for the Lama Lama community that supports long-term community growth and improves quality of life?

The Lama Lama community is currently experiencing ongoing energy challenges due to inconsistent funding and limited energy infrastructure. Existing systems are unable to consistently meet the community’s energy demands, which restricts economic opportunities, tourism, and overall development. Limited access to reliable power also impacts living standards and reduces opportunities for long-term growth within the community. The ideal outcome is a dependable and maintainable energy solution that provides stable electricity, supports local development, encourages economic activity, and improves the overall wellbeing of the Lama Lama people.

3.1 Design Criteria

Criterion 1: Energy Reliability

The solution must provide a stable and consistent supply of electricity throughout the day with minimal outages or interruptions. Reliability will be assessed based on the system’s ability to meet community energy demands during normal daily operation. This is important because unreliable energy directly affects homes, businesses, and essential services within the community.

Criterion 2: Low long-term costs

The solution must remain affordable to operate and maintain over time, with manageable maintenance and running costs. Assessment will focus on installation costs, operational expenses, and long-term financial sustainability. This is important because inconsistent funding is one of the key causes of the current energy issues faced by the community.

Criterion 3: Environmental Impact

The solution should minimise environmental damage by reducing reliance on high-pollution energy sources and using cleaner renewable alternatives where possible. Environmental impact will be assessed through energy efficiency and reduction of emissions. This is important to help preserve the local environment and ensure the solution remains viable for future generations.

Criterion 4: Community Benefit

The solution should improve quality of life by supporting local businesses, tourism, education, and community services. Success will be measured through the potential social and economic benefits created by the energy system. This is important because the project should contribute to long-term community development rather than only solving short-term energy shortages.

Criterion 5: Ease of Maintenance

The system should be simple enough to maintain with available skills, resources, and equipment. Maintenance requirements will be assessed based on repair complexity, required training, and access to replacement components. This is important because difficult or expensive maintenance could make the solution unsustainable for the community over time.

Section 6

Detailed Design

The selected design — a hybrid smart energy management system — integrates new solar and battery infrastructure with the existing solar array and diesel generator, guided by a real-time AI-driven monitoring and balancing system. The design philosophy was shaped by both Lama Lama community Values and EWB design brief prioritising long lifecycle interconnected design and cost (EWB Challenge Design Considerations, 2026).

System Components

The final proposed design consists of the following key components:

  • Monocrystalline solar panels — new high-efficiency panels supplementing the existing array
  • LiFePO₄ (lithium iron phosphate) battery array — new primary battery storage with long cycle life and thermal stability
  • MPPT charge controller — maximises energy harvested from the solar array
  • Inverter — converts DC stored energy to AC for appliance use
  • DC bus / combiner — integrates multiple energy sources onto a common bus
  • Bypass switch — allows direct solar-to-inverter routing when batteries are full
  • Smart energy monitoring and balancing system — the core intelligence layer of the design

Redundancy & Resilience

A key strength of this design is its multiple layers of redundancy. The system is capable of drawing power from three independent sources, in order of preference:

  • 1. New LiFePO₄ battery array — primary storage, optimised for efficiency
  • 2. Old battery system — retained as secondary storage and backup
  • 3. Diesel generator — emergency fallback, with significantly reduced usage compared to the original system

This layered approach creates a highly resilient system suited to the remote conditions of Lama Lama Country, where maintenance access is limited and downtime is unacceptable.

Original System — Before Upgrade

Figure 1 below illustrates the existing energy system prior to the proposed upgrade. Energy flows from the solar array and diesel generator through a simple battery-charged decision — if the battery is charged, power goes directly to appliances; if not, the generator is activated. This system lacks any intelligent optimisation, resulting in inefficient energy use and heavy reliance on the diesel generator.

How the Smart System Works

This is the key part of the system that sets it apart and makes it the most practical for the Lama Lama energy needs.

The smart monitoring systems core component is a set of mirrored computers (onsite and offsite). That provide an output based on the following inputs:

  • Battery information — current state of charge, tempreture, cycle count
  • Current energy generation — live solar input
  • Number of users — active load on the system
  • Past energy usage patterns — historical data of energy usage
  • Weather — forcast for predicting solar yield
  • Time of day — anticipating peak and off-peak demand periods

Using this data, the algorithm makes two key real-time decisions:

1. Solar Power Distribution — Where to Put Incoming Energy

The system selects the optimal destination for incoming solar power, choosing from:

  • New LiFePO₄ battery array (primary storage)
  • Old battery system (secondary storage)
  • Bypass directly to inverter (when batteries are full)

2. Energy Supply Selection — Where to Draw Power From

This system choices which of the folowing is the optimal sources to take energyfrom:

  • New LiFePO₄ battery array
  • Old battery array
  • Direct from solar array
  • Diesel generator (emergency backup only)

Additionally, the system sends notifications to the community ranger’s informing them of predicted energy lulls and surpluses, or when the generator is activated, enabling proactive maintenance and oversight.

The result is a system that delivers improved efficiency through intelligent prioritisation, greater adaptation to changing conditions, improved longevity of all components through optimised charge cycles, and better utilisation of all available energy resources — both new and existing.

To summarise the smart monitoring system. It takes in all relevant information and then directs the system on the best way to use the energy

Community Concerns & Responses

  • Cultural appropriateness: The design was guided by the Lama Lama community's value of respect to the land and sea. By prioritising renewable solar energy, the design respects and reflects this ethos. The continued presence of the diesel generator is acknowledged as a limitation, but its usage will be dramatically reduced. (LamaLama About Us, 2026)
  • Maintenance & reliability: The layered redundancy (new battery → old battery → generator) combined with the smart energy distribution ensures the community is never left completely without power. The mirrored on-site/off-site computer system provides resilience against hardware failure. Ranger notifications enable early intervention before issues escalate
  • Cost: By integrating with existing infrastructure rather than replacing it, significant capital costs are avoided. The smart system reduces generator runtime, lowering ongoing fuel costs substantially over time. It also features modularity and expansion allowing for minimal upfront cost.
  • Environmental impact: Diesel generator usage is dramatically reduced through intelligent prioritisation of solar and battery sources. While not fully eliminating diesel, the system offers a substantial step toward cleaner energy for the community.
